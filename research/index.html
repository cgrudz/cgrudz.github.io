Chaotic dynamics are often described in terms of the “butterfly effect” where a minor change in the initialization of a model, tantamount to the flap of a butterfly wing, produces a radically different outcome over the course of a model run. My research interests lie primarily in the intersection of data, stability and prediction, to understand how dynamic instability impacts the uncertainty of predictive models. My postdoctoral appointment at NERSC is funded by the Reduced subspace in big data treatment: A new paradigm for efficient geophysical Data Assimilation (REDDA) project. Please see preprints and computational resources available below.

Data Assimilation

It is well known in the weather forecasting community dynamic instability is among the primary drivers of forecast uncertainty in chaotic, physical systems and a wide variety of data assimilation techniques are designed exploit this phenomena. Along with others, my work has have provided formal proofs of the central hypothesis of the Assimilation in the Unstable Subspace methodology of Anna Trevisan and her collaborators: for filters in perfect, linear models, the distribution of forecast errors asymptotically conforms to the unstable-neutral subspace, i.e., the column span of the error covariance aligns with the span of backward Lyapunov vectors with non-negative exponents. In my current research, I am extending this framework and methodology to filter design in the presence of model errors and non-linear error evolution.

Smart Grids

As part of my graduate research training, I was an intern at Los Alamos National Labs where I performed data analysis and network reduction for electric grid transmission networks. I am interested in the application of data assimilation and machine learning techniques for parameter and state estimation for dynamic electric grid models; the expansion of renewable generation has brought many opportunities but also uncertainties in electric generation, and data driven prediction techniques offer methodology for optimization and grid security under uncertainty.

Stability Analysis

While PDEs are not generally analytically solvable, coherent states such as waves and solitons can provide useful qualitative information about the system of equations. Stable states in particular attract other model solutions in their asymptotic state, and provide model solutions that are robust in realistic, noisy settings. Determining the stability of coherent states is often performed with Evans function analysis, and in addition my work on data assimilation, my graduate thesis proved a novel formualtion of the Evans function for reaction diffusion equations.
