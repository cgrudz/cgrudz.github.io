ORCHID Profile

In physical applications, dynamical models and observational data play dual roles, each representing sources of incomplete and inaccurate information. In data rich problems, first-principle physical laws constrain the degrees of freedom of massive data sets, utilizing our prior insights to complex processes. Respectively, in data sparse problems, dynamical models fill spatial and temporal gaps in observational networks. My research interest lie in the intesection of data and models, utilizing the dynamical and statistical properties of physical systems for prediction and uncertainty quantification.

Ongoing and past projects

Data assimilation broadly refers to the techniques used to combine model and observational information to produce an optimal estimate of a PDF or a test statistic. These techniques include methods from Bayesian inference, dynamical systems, numerical analysis and optimal control. In phsyical applications such as weather prediction, the butterfly effect renders the forecasting problem inherently volatile; however, chaotic dynamics also put strong constraints on the evolution of errors. It is well understood in the weather prediction community that the growth of forecast uncertainty is confined to a much lower dimensional subspace corresponding to the directions of rapidly growing perturbations --- this is characterized by the unstable-neutral manifold of the state being tracked, with dimension equal to the number of non-negative Lyapunov exponents, leading to dimensional reduction of the uncertainty quantification problem.

With my collaborators, I am studying the mathematical foundations of ensemble based forecasting and stochastic filtering in the context of smooth and random dynamical systems. We have produced refined necessary and sufficient conditions for filter convergence and stability, rigorously establishing dimensional reduction methodology and a novel theoretical framework for the ensemble Kalman filter in geophysical applications. Our ongoing research seeks to provide new insights into filter design and uncertainty quantification for physical systems.

The expansion of renewable generation has brought many opportunities but also uncertainties in electricy generation. Simultaneously, the expansion of new grid management technologies and the internet of things have offered new possibilities for data driven prediction and optimization for grid security under uncertainty. Purely statistical machine learning techniques are particularly useful in prediction where data is abundant and models of the process are inferior to observations. However, neither is the case for the electric grid where Phasor Measurement Units (PMUs) offer spatially sparse measurements and electric transmission is guided by physical power flow laws. For these reasons, the rigorous incorporation of nonlinear, time varying process models is strong advantage of data assimilation methodology for the problems of state estimation and fault detection in the electric grid. I was a graduate research assistant at Los Alamos National Labs where I performed data analysis and network reduction for electric grid transmission networks. Our ongoing collaboration is now focused on the application of data assimilation and machine learning techniques for parameter and state estimation for dynamic electric grid models.

While PDEs are not generally analytically solvable, coherent states such as waves and solitons can provide useful qualitative information about the system of equations. Stable states in particular attract other model solutions in their asymptotic state, and provide model solutions that are robust in realistic, noisy settings. Determining the stability of coherent states is often performed with Evans function analysis, and in addition my work on data assimilation, my graduate thesis proved a novel formualtion of the Evans function for reaction diffusion equations.
